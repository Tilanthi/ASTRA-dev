PART II: ASSESSMENT OF DISCOVERY AND INFERENCE CAPABILITIES — CAN THEY BE BETTER DEMONSTRATED?
The authors have asked for a frank assessment of whether the current test suite convincingly demonstrates discovery and inference capabilities, and what changes to the architecture or test design would provide stronger evidence. This forms an extended commentary intended to inform future development and a potential follow-up paper.

1. THE CORE PROBLEM WITH THE CURRENT TEST SUITE
The honest assessment is that the five test cases, taken collectively, demonstrate that ASTRA correctly implements a set of established analysis algorithms and applies them to real data. This is valuable and worth publishing, but it falls short of demonstrating the discovery and causal inference capabilities that the system name and architecture imply.
The specific shortfall is this: in every test case, the correct answer is known in advance. The virial theorem governs filament scaling relations. The CDFS sources are well-characterised in the literature. The galaxy correlations are textbook results. The Gaia causal structure is definitional. The power-law model is theoretically motivated. ASTRA recovers all of these, which validates implementation, but does not demonstrate that the system can identify something genuinely unknown.
This is not a criticism of the current paper, which explicitly and commendably acknowledges these limitations. It is rather an identification of what would be needed in a follow-up paper to make the stronger discovery claim.

2. WHAT GENUINE DISCOVERY AND INFERENCE DEMONSTRATION WOULD REQUIRE
A convincing demonstration of discovery capability has three necessary properties. The target relationship must not be pre-encoded in the system's knowledge base. The result must be statistically significant and physically interpretable. And the result must be verifiable — either against subsequently published data or against held-out observational data not used in the discovery analysis.
A convincing demonstration of causal inference capability requires going beyond textbook relationships. The causal structure must be non-obvious, with plausible competing explanations, and the inference must distinguish between them in a way that adds scientific value.

3. SPECIFIC TEST CASES THAT WOULD DEMONSTRATE THESE CAPABILITIES
3.1 A Genuine Causal Inference Problem: Star Formation Threshold Identification
The relationship between gas column density and star formation activity has a well-known threshold around N(H₂) ~ 10²¹ cm⁻², but the causal drivers of this threshold — whether it reflects gravitational instability, magnetic field decoupling, or dust shielding of CO — remain debated. A test case applying ASTRA's PC algorithm to a multi-cloud dataset combining column density, magnetic field proxy (dust polarisation fraction), CO line width, and star formation tracer (YSO counts or 70 µm point sources) would constitute a genuine causal inference problem with unknown ground truth.
The key requirement is that at least one of the causal pathways must be non-obvious: specifically, whether the N(H₂) threshold is a cause of star formation or a confounded correlation with the true causal variable (e.g., Jeans instability or magnetic critical mass). If ASTRA correctly identifies the confounding structure — for example, recovering that column density and star formation rate are both effects of a common gravitational cause, rather than column density causing star formation — this would be a scientifically interesting result that cannot be read off from domain knowledge alone.
Data for this test are publicly available: the Herschel HOBYS survey combined with Spitzer c2d and Gould Belt Legacy surveys provide the required multi-property catalogues for several molecular clouds.

3.2 A Discovery Test: Anomalous Scaling in Evolved Stellar Populations
The period-luminosity relations for RR Lyrae and Cepheid variables are well-characterised in standard photometric bands, but their behaviour in the mid-infrared — particularly the sensitivity of the slope to metallicity and environment — contains unresolved scatter whose causal origin is debated. A test applying ASTRA's dimensional analysis and functional form discovery to a combined WISE/2MASS/Gaia dataset for a population of RR Lyrae variables in a globular cluster with well-characterised metallicity spread would constitute a genuine discovery test.
The specific target would be whether ASTRA autonomously identifies the metallicity dependence of the period-luminosity slope without being prompted to look for it. If ASTRA's anomaly detection flags the metallicity-correlated residuals and its pattern recognition connects them to the known metallicity-dependent instability strip width, this would demonstrate genuine autonomous pattern recognition beyond template retrieval — because the specific quantitative form of the metallicity correction in mid-infrared bands is not settled in the literature and would not be pre-encoded in the knowledge base.

3.3 A Multi-Scale Causal Inference Problem: AGN Feedback in Galaxy Groups
The causal relationship between AGN activity and star formation quenching in group environments involves three competing mechanisms — thermal feedback, kinetic feedback, and tidal stripping — that produce overlapping observational signatures. A test applying ASTRA's structural causal model discovery to a sample of X-ray-detected galaxy groups with combined Chandra (AGN power), XMM-Newton (hot gas properties), and optical spectroscopy (star formation rates and stellar ages) would probe a genuinely contested causal structure.
The test would be designed so that ASTRA's domain knowledge encodes the three competing mechanisms as prior hypotheses but does not specify their relative importance or the expected causal graph structure. A successful outcome would be ASTRA identifying which mechanism is causally dominant in the data, with the result validated against published multi-wavelength studies of the same groups. This constitutes a verifiable discovery test: the ground truth is known from external literature that can be withheld during the analysis.

3.4 A Held-Out Prediction Test: The Correct Structure for a Genuinely Novel Dataset
The most convincing demonstration architecture is a prospective blind test. ASTRA generates a causal model or scaling relation prediction from a training dataset. The prediction is registered before new data are collected or analysed. The new data then provide a test of whether the prediction was correct.
A practical version: use ASTRA to characterise the scaling relations and causal structure of filaments in the Herschel HGBS survey clouds (the current dataset). Then apply the identified relations as predictions to ALMA high-resolution observations of the same filaments, which probe different spatial scales and would violate the predictions if the scaling relation breaks at sub-parsec scales. If ASTRA's predictions hold at ALMA resolution, this constitutes a genuine prospective validation. If they fail, ASTRA should identify why — demonstrating the meta-critical capability discussed in earlier versions of the paper.

4. ARCHITECTURAL ENHANCEMENTS THAT WOULD STRENGTHEN THE DISCOVERY CLAIM
4.1 Knowledge Base Transparency
The current architecture includes a MORK ontology and domain knowledge system, but the paper does not specify what is and is not encoded in it. This creates a fundamental ambiguity: when ASTRA identifies a pattern, a referee cannot determine whether this is genuine discovery or retrieval from encoded prior knowledge. For discovery claims to be credible, the knowledge base must be explicitly bounded. The system should be able to report, for any identified relationship, whether that relationship was pre-encoded or identified from data.
This requires adding a provenance flag to every pattern identified: "retrieved from domain knowledge" versus "identified from data, not pre-encoded." Without this flag, all pattern identification results are ambiguous. Implementing this would also provide the basis for the claim in Section 5.3 — that genuine discovery requires identifying patterns not already in the knowledge base — to be operationalised rather than merely stated.

4.2 A Formal Novelty Score
Related to the above: the system would benefit from a formal novelty metric that quantifies how expected a discovered relationship is given the prior knowledge state. One approach is a likelihood ratio: P(observing this pattern | domain knowledge) versus P(observing this pattern | data alone). A low prior probability combined with a high posterior probability constitutes a genuine discovery signal.
This is not a trivial addition, but it transforms the discovery capability from a qualitative claim into a measurable property. Papers that implement such a metric would be substantially more convincing than those that rely on human judgement about whether a result is novel.

4.3 Confounded Causal Inference with Latent Variables
The current causal inference implementation uses the PC algorithm, which assumes causal sufficiency — no unmeasured common causes. This is appropriate for the textbook Gaia demonstration, where the variables are well-defined and their relationships are definitional. For genuine astrophysical causal inference, this assumption is almost always violated. The FCI algorithm (also implemented in the causal-learn library already cited) handles latent confounders and produces partial ancestral graphs that explicitly represent uncertainty about causal direction when confounders may be present.
Adding FCI as an alternative to PC, with automatic selection based on a test for causal sufficiency, would substantially strengthen the causal inference capability. More importantly, demonstrating FCI on a problem with a known latent confounder — for example, the well-known confounding of stellar mass and environment in galaxy evolution, where halo mass is the latent common cause — would provide a compelling test case that genuinely demonstrates causal inference beyond correlation detection.

4.4 Cross-Domain Transfer as a Test
The architecture claims 75 domain modules with cross-domain knowledge transfer capability, but no test in the paper exercises this. A test that explicitly uses knowledge learned in one domain to make predictions in another would constitute a genuine architectural demonstration. For example, the virial scaling relations characterised in Test Case 1 could be applied as prior expectations for turbulent linewidth-mass relations in protostellar cores — a different scale regime — to test whether the cross-domain transfer improves prediction accuracy compared to fitting the core data independently.
This is the kind of test that would distinguish ASTRA from a collection of well-implemented single-domain tools wrapped in a common interface.

5. SUMMARY RECOMMENDATION FOR IMPROVING THE PAPER
An improved paper demonstrating genuine discovery and inference capability should include: at least one test case where the correct answer is not pre-encoded in the knowledge base; a formal distinction between knowledge retrieval and data-driven discovery; a prospective or held-out validation against data not used in the analysis; a causal inference test using FCI on a problem with known latent confounders; and a cross-domain transfer test exercising the 75-domain architecture.
