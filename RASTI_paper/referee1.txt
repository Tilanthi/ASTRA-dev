EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This revised manuscript represents a substantial improvement over what I understand would be a typical first submission in this area. The authors have made commendable choices throughout: the claims are calibrated to what is actually demonstrated, limitations are acknowledged explicitly and at point of first reporting rather than buried in conclusions, and the framing of individual test cases as capability demonstrations rather than novel discoveries is scientifically honest and appropriate. The paper is now coherent, internally consistent, and makes a defensible contribution to the RASTI readership. The remaining issues are genuine but addressable within a minor revision cycle, and none requires fundamental restructuring of the paper's argument.

SECTION 1: REMAINING CONCERNS REQUIRING ATTENTION
1.1 The GitHub Repository and Reproducibility
The paper relies on a GitHub repository for complete source code, extended test cases, and reproducible notebooks, cited as White & Dey (2026). This is standard practice for software papers, and RASTI accepts it. However, two issues remain.
First, the repository URL appears in both the text and the reference list but the reference list entry reads only as a URL with no additional bibliographic detail. RASTI requires software citations to include at minimum a version identifier, release date, or DOI. The authors should either register a version-locked release via Zenodo (which generates a citable DOI), or tag a specific commit and report the commit hash alongside the URL. Without this, the results cannot be definitively reproduced from a fixed system state.
Second, the paper states the repository includes "reproducible code for all results presented in this paper." A referee cannot verify this claim during review. The authors should confirm in the manuscript that all five test cases can be reproduced by running specific named notebooks in the repository, and should name those notebooks explicitly, even if only in a footnote or supplementary table.

1.2 The Positioning Relative to LLMs (Section 2.6) Still Contains an Inaccuracy
Section 2.6 retains the statement: "Unlike large language models that process text but cannot analyze numerical data, ASTRA directly processes astronomical catalogs, time series, and images." This characterisation, while improved from earlier versions, remains inaccurate for frontier models with code execution capability at the time of submission. GPT-4 with Code Interpreter and Claude with code execution both process FITS catalogs, execute Python, and perform numerical analysis directly. The distinction the authors are actually drawing — and which is scientifically defensible — is between LLMs that require explicit programming and manual tool orchestration versus ASTRA's integrated framework with native physical validation. That is a genuine and meaningful difference worth stating, but the current wording will draw objection from any referee with familiarity with current LLM capabilities.
Recommended replacement framing: "Unlike large language models that require explicit programming and manual tool selection to perform numerical analysis, and do not natively integrate physical validation constraints or causal interpretation, ASTRA provides these capabilities within a unified framework." This is both accurate and more persuasive because it identifies the genuine integration advantage rather than a capability gap that does not exist.

1.3 Test Case 1: The Filament Sample and Distance Assumptions
Section 3.1 states the 24 filaments are "drawn from the publicly released filament catalogue (Arzoumanian et al. 2019)" with "distance estimates based on nearby Galactic OB associations." This is acceptable as a general statement but the filament virial analysis is sensitive to distance in a specific way that requires more explicit treatment.
The velocity dispersion scales as σv ∝ (M/L)^α, where M depends on distance squared and L depends on distance linearly, making M/L ∝ distance. A systematic distance uncertainty of 20% — typical for kinematic distances in molecular cloud complexes — propagates to a ~20% systematic uncertainty in M/L, comparable to the 12% discrepancy between measured and theoretical virial slopes. The 2.7σ tension identified in Section 3.3 and discussed in Section 7.3 is correctly flagged as potentially due to systematic distance uncertainties, but the paper should quantify this: what is the typical distance uncertainty for these 24 filaments, and what is the implied systematic uncertainty in the virial slope comparison? If the distance uncertainties alone can account for the observed tension, this should be stated explicitly. If they cannot, this is an interesting physical result.
This is not a fatal flaw — the tension is correctly reported and discussed — but the physical interpretation would be significantly strengthened by this quantification.

1.4 Test Case 2: The 60/370 Source Selection Needs Clarification
The paper now correctly reports that 60 of approximately 370 X-ray sources have secure detections in all three wavebands, representing about 16% of the X-ray catalogue. The selection effect explanation is provided: the remaining sources are primarily high-redshift AGN without optical/IR counterparts, or star-forming galaxies below the X-ray flux limit.
However, the claim that the selected 60 sources represent "secure detections in all three wavebands" raises a question about how "secure" is defined. The paper adopts a posterior match probability threshold of >0.9 for cross-matching. Were the 60 sources selected purely on this probabilistic criterion, or were additional quality cuts applied (signal-to-noise thresholds in individual bands, rejection of extended sources, etc.)? The answer affects whether the classified sample of 41 AGN and 19 stars is representative of the CDFS X-ray source population at all redshifts, or whether it is biased toward bright, compact, relatively low-redshift sources. This selection bias should be explicitly acknowledged in Section 4.3, even if briefly.
Additionally, Figure 2 still carries embedded capability comparison text panels inherited from earlier versions of the paper. The figure caption and main text have been updated appropriately, but the embedded panels within the figure itself (visible at full resolution) still contain the misleading LLM comparison claims from the previous version. These should be removed from the figure or replaced with neutral descriptive text.

1.5 Test Case 3: The SDSS Dataset Specification
The dataset is now correctly identified as "drawn from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey Data Release 7 (Abazajian et al. 2009)." This is a significant improvement. However, the paper does not cite Abazajian et al. (2009) in the reference list — it appears in the text but is absent from the references. Additionally, the selection criteria for the 600-galaxy sample are not fully specified. The paper states the galaxies are "selected to have complete measurements of stellar mass, star formation rate, gas-phase metallicity, and local galaxy density" at z < 0.2, which is a reasonable selection, but does not specify which SDSS spectroscopic catalogue provides the SFR and metallicity estimates. These are derived quantities requiring specific pipeline choices: SDSS DR7 SFRs from the MPA-JHU catalogue (Brinchmann et al. 2004) are the standard, but this should be stated. Without this, the 600-galaxy sample cannot be reproduced or independently verified.

1.6 Test Case 5: The 88% versus 72% Agreement Discrepancy
Section 7.2 explains the difference between the 88% slope ratio and the 72% dimensional analysis agreement as "different normalization choices for the comparison." This explanation is provided for the first time in Section 7 — it does not appear in Section 3 where the 88% figure is first introduced, leaving a reader who reads sequentially confused about the inconsistency between the two numbers until they reach Section 7. The brief reconciliation should be moved forward to Section 3.3, or at minimum a forward reference added in Section 3.3 pointing to Section 7.2 for the reconciliation.
The dimensional analysis comparison itself (Π_measured = 1.012 ± 0.087 vs Π_theory = √2 = 1.414) is interesting but the physical meaning of the dimensionless parameter Π is not defined in the text. What combination of filament properties does this represent? A reader unfamiliar with the specific dimensionless form of the virial theorem for cylindrical filaments cannot evaluate this comparison without additional context. One sentence defining Π would suffice.

SECTION 2: MINOR CONCERNS
2.1 Reference List Gaps
Beyond the missing Abazajian et al. (2009) citation noted above, the reference list has several gaps for a paper of this scope. The Spirtes, Glymour & Scheines (2000) reference is listed without publisher details. The Sutherland & Saunders (1992) reference for the Bayesian cross-matching methodology is cited in the text but absent from the reference list. Lotz et al. (2011) is cited in Section 5.2 for merger rate evolution but does not appear in the references. Mo & White (1996) is cited for the halo model framework but is similarly absent. Meng & Wong (1996) for bridge sampling is cited in Section 7.1 but absent from the references. Hornschemeier et al. (2001) is cited in Section 4.1 but absent. The Gaia DR2 data paper (Gaia Collaboration, Brown et al. 2018, A&A, 616, A1) should be cited for the Gaia dataset used in Test Case 4.
These are straightforward additions but their current absence will require a revision round regardless of other issues.

2.2 Figure Labels and Embedded Text
As noted above, Figure 2 retains figure-panel embedded text that appears to be carried over from an earlier version. More broadly, Figures 1 and 4 still carry labels reading "Test 2" and "Test 11" respectively in their embedded panels, which do not correspond to any section or test number in the current paper. These should be updated to match the current section numbering or removed. These are clearly artefacts from the broader 15-test-case evaluation suite referred to in the GitHub repository, not from this paper's five test cases.

2.3 Section 1.1 Scope and Limitations Repetition
Section 1.1 restates that ASTRA "does not claim general reasoning beyond its training domains or autonomous operation without human oversight." This is appropriate and welcome. However, the identical framing — "what ASTRA demonstrates" versus "what ASTRA does not claim" — appears verbatim in both Section 1.1 and Section 8.1, which creates redundancy. The Section 8.1 version adds no new information beyond Section 1.1. Consider either removing the Section 8.1 repetition or using the Discussion section to reflect on what the five test cases collectively revealed about the system's strengths and limitations, rather than restating the scope statement.

2.4 The 16-Capability List in Section 2.3
Section 2.3 lists sixteen distinct analytical capabilities. Of these, only a subset are actually demonstrated in the five test cases: scaling relations discovery (capability 2), causal inference (capability 3), model selection (capability 4), multi-wavelength fusion (capability 5), uncertainty quantification (capability 6), hypothesis generation (capability 9 — implicitly via pattern recognition), anomaly detection (capability 14), and physical validation (capability 16). Capabilities including temporal reasoning (capability 7), instrument-aware analysis (capability 8), analogical reasoning (capability 10), counterfactual analysis (capability 11), and ensemble prediction (capability 15) are described but not demonstrated in this paper.
This is not inherently problematic — a software paper can describe capabilities not all demonstrated in a single paper — but the text does not clearly distinguish between capabilities demonstrated in this paper and those available in the system but demonstrated elsewhere (e.g., in the extended test cases in the GitHub repository). Adding a brief note to Section 2.3, or a summary table, indicating which capabilities are demonstrated in which test cases would significantly improve clarity.

2.5 Bootstrap Uncertainty on the Virial Slope
Section 3.1 states that bootstrap confidence intervals with 10,000 resamples are used for uncertainty quantification. The reported uncertainty on the virial slope is ±0.0043. For N=24 data points, a bootstrap uncertainty of this magnitude is plausible, but the paper should confirm whether the bootstrap was performed on the original data points or on residuals from the fit (parametric versus non-parametric bootstrap), as the choice can matter for small samples. Additionally, for a power-law fit to data spanning roughly a factor of 5 in M/L, it would be useful to confirm that the fit was performed in log-log space (as is standard for power law fitting) rather than linear space, since linear fitting of a power law relationship introduces bias. The text does not specify this.

SECTION 3: POSITIVE ASSESSMENT
This version of the paper demonstrates genuine improvement in scientific integrity and clarity. Several specific improvements deserve explicit acknowledgement.
The honest reporting of the 2.7σ tension in Test Case 1, with physical interpretation of possible causes rather than a simple "88% agreement" claim, is exactly the standard expected of a RASTI methods paper.
The explanation of why zero galaxies appear in the CDFS matched sample (Section 4.2) directly addresses a question that would otherwise undermine confidence in the analysis. The response is physically well-reasoned and consistent with the established CDFS literature.
The explicit acknowledgement in Test Cases 3 and 4 that the demonstrated capabilities are pattern recovery and implementation validation respectively, not novel discovery, sets an appropriate standard for scientific honesty. The statement in Section 5.3 — "This is a demonstration of pattern recognition and knowledge retrieval from encoded domain knowledge, not novel discovery" — is notably candid and appropriate.
The Bayesian model selection section now correctly and prominently reports that the power-law and logarithmic models are statistically indistinguishable, using the Kass-Raftery scale explicitly. Table 1 with the "Statistically indistinguishable" interpretation column is a clean and honest presentation.
The updated LLM positioning in Section 2.6 is substantially more accurate than previous versions, though one remaining inaccuracy is flagged above.
